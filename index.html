Executive Brief
The Golden Era: India–Saudi corridor grew to 3.4M departures in 2024, the #2 destination for Indian outbound travellers. Non-religious tourism surged 108% vs 2019.
The Turning Point: October 7, 2023 — Israel-Hamas conflict collapsed Middle East travel sentiment overnight. US-Iran escalation in Feb 2026 delivered the next severe shock.
The Decline: 5,000+ flights cancelled in 2 days of Feb 2026 escalation. Middle East inbound arrivals forecast at −11% to −27% for 2026. Indian aviation capacity to Middle East down 7.9%.
The Resilience: Saudi Arabia hit 122M total visitors in 2025, revised Vision 2030 target upward to 150M. Religious tourism (Hajj/Umrah) remains structurally inelastic.

Primary catalyst for regional destabilisation. Travel Sentiment Score for the Middle East plummeted to COVID-era lows overnight. Initial cancellations concentrated in long-haul leisure from North America and Europe. Religious tourism from India initially insulated.

"Two MENAs" Divergence
Jordan, Lebanon saw immediate decline. GCC (incl. Saudi Arabia) initially maintained stability perception. However Indian leisure forward booking curves began softening — "wait-and-see" approach delayed summer season purchases.

India MEA Travel Advisory Issued
Government of India issued official advisory for all Indian nationals to avoid non-essential travel to Iran and Israel. First formal government signal of elevated risk — directly suppressed leisure demand from Indian middle-class travellers.

Mid-2024
Iran Retaliatory Strikes — Gulf Fear Peaks
Iranian missile and drone strikes on Israel triggered the first serious GCC safety concern. Non-GCC Middle Eastern countries received 2.35 million fewer European visitors in first 9 months of 2024 vs 2023. North Africa attracted 2.75M more — direct substitution effect confirmed.

US-Iran Conflict — Worst Aviation Shock
Most severe escalation. Over 5,000 commercial flights cancelled within first 2 days. WTTC estimated the conflict was costing the global Travel & Tourism sector at least $600M per day. Middle Eastern airports handle ~14% of global transit — systemic disruption unavoidable.

Air India & IndiGo Issue Flight Advisories
Air India and IndiGo issued April 6 advisory on limited flights to Dubai, Abu Dhabi, Riyadh and Jeddah. Indian overall international airline capacity dropped 7.9% in April 2026 — driven specifically by weaker demand on Middle Eastern routes.

⚠
The "Neighbourhood Effect" — India's Specific Risk
UN Tourism identifies a documented "Neighbourhood Effect" in conflict tourism: even countries physically untouched by conflict suffer dramatically if they share perceived regional proximity with the affected zone. For Indian leisure travellers — who are highly price-sensitive and rely on aggregated travel advisories — Saudi Arabia is perceived as part of the broader "Middle East conflict zone" regardless of its physical safety. This psychological barrier is the single hardest element to reverse through marketing alone.
UN Tourism Brief, Apr 15, 2026

🔍
Most Analogous Case: 9/11 (2001)
The current India–Saudi decline most closely mirrors the post-9/11 crisis: (1) Psychological proximity — travellers avoiding an entire region despite specific sites being safe; (2) Aviation disruption — capacity cuts driven by fear, not physical access; (3) India-specific hesitation — price-sensitive middle-market travellers substitute destinations rather than delay. Post-9/11 recovery took ~18 months. Under a base-case resolution scenario, full India–Saudi flow recovery is projected by Q3–Q4 2027.

Religious tourism is the anchor: Hajj and Umrah pilgrims represent entirely inelastic demand. This segment will resume the moment airspace is confirmed open — providing an immediate floor under Saudi Arabia's India-inbound volumes regardless of leisure hesitation.
The 5–6 month rule (Scenario C): UN Tourism models show that once kinetic conflict fully ceases, even deeply psychologically deterred markets like India typically normalise within 5–6 months — provided active re-engagement campaigns are deployed at the precise moment hostilities end.
Ticket price is the hidden variable: Indian middle-income travellers are highly price-elastic. Jet fuel surcharge rerouting costs will keep ticket prices elevated even post-ceasefire — potentially delaying leisure recovery beyond sentiment normalisation.
